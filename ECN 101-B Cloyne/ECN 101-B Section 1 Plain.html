Growth Rates

We may consider the growth rate of variables. We use the Greek letter \(\Delta\) (“Delta”) to signal change:

\[\%\Delta X=\frac{\Delta X}{X}=\frac{X_{new}-X_{old}}{X_{old}}\]

Level vs. Growth Rate: It is important to distinguish between the level of a variable and its growth rate. The level of X is its actual value at a given point in time (e.g., GDP = $28 trillion). The growth rate of X measures how quickly that value is changing (e.g., GDP grew 2.8% last year).

Properties of the Natural Log

We can use the natural logarithm (\(\ln\)) for:

  • Approximating percent change: \(\Delta\ln(X)\approx\frac{\Delta X}{X}\)

  • Breaking up multiplication: \(\ln(XY)=\ln(X)+\ln(Y)\)

  • …or division: \(\ln(X/Y)=\ln(X)-\ln(Y)\)

  • Breaking up exponents \(\ln(X^Y) = Y\times\ln(X)\)

Growth Rate Rules

Using the log properties from the previous slide, we can derive three handy rules (where \(g_X\) denotes the growth rate of \(X\)):

  • Product rule: \(g_{XY} = g_X + g_Y\)
  • Quotient rule: \(g_{X/Y} = g_X - g_Y\)
  • Power rule: \(g_{X^a} = a \cdot g_X\)

These rules will appear throughout the course — especially when we work with production functions like \(Y = K^\alpha L^{1-\alpha}\) in the Solow model.

Revenue Growth Example

For example, we know \(Revenue=Price\times Quantity\).

If quantity grew by 5% last year and price grew by 10%, by how much did revenue grow?

\[\begin{align*} g_R &= g_P + g_Q\\ &= 10\% + 5\%\\ &= 15\% \end{align*}\]

We can see that revenue grew by approximately 15% last year.

Derivatives

We may also want to find the derivative, or instantaneous rate of change, of a function \(y=f(x)\). We can write this:

  • \(f'(x)\)
  • \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) or \(\frac{d}{dx}[f(x)]\)
  • \(f_x(x)\)

We express a derivative as: \[f'(x)=\underset{\Delta x\rightarrow0}{lim}\frac{f(x+\Delta x)-f(x)}{\Delta x}\]

Derivative Rules

Some common rules for derivatives we will take:

  • Constant rule: derivative of a constant ( \(k\) ) is zero: \(\frac{d}{dx}[k]=0\)
  • Power rule: derivative of an exponent: \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^n]=nx^{n-1}\)
  • Product rule: derivative of a product: \(\frac{d}{dx}[f(x)g(x)]=f'(x)g(x)+f(x)g'(x)\)
  • Chain rule: derivative of nested functions: \(\frac{d}{dx}g(f(x))=g'(f(x))\times f'(x)\)

Partial Derivatives

Additionally, we may want to take derivatives of functions with multiple input variables, \(f(x,y)\) . In this case, we would take partial derivatives, where we hold all other variables constant. We use \(\partial\) (“partial”) rather than \(d\) (“total”) here:

  • With respect to X holding Y constant: \(\frac{\partial}{\partial x}f(x,y)=f_x(x,y)\)
  • With respect to Y holding X constant: \(\frac{\partial}{\partial y}f(x,y)=f_y(x,y)\)
